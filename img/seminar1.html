def consumption_ss(gamma,n=n,alpha=alpha,delta=delta,g=0,A=A,h=h):
    k = h*(gamma*A/(delta+n+g))**(1/(1-alpha))
    y = A*k**alpha*h**(1-alpha)
    c = (1-gamma)*y
    return c

Golden Rule of Savings Rate¶

At the steady state, consumption is equal to:

$$ c^\ast = (1-\gamma) y^\ast $$$$ c^\ast = (1-\gamma) A^{1/(1-\alpha)} \left(\frac{\gamma}{\delta+n}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}h $$

What is the saving rates that maximizes steady state consumption?

$$\max_{\gamma} \left\{(1-\gamma) A^{1/(1-\alpha)} \left(\frac{\gamma}{\delta+n}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}h \right\}$$

Technical note: Under certain conditions, the value of $\gamma$ that sets the derivative of this function equal to zero is the $\gamma$ value that maximizes the function.

$$ \frac{dc}{d\gamma} = - A^{1/(1-\alpha)} \left(\frac{\gamma}{\delta+n}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}h + (1-\gamma)\frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\frac{1}{\gamma} A^{1/(1-\alpha)} \left(\frac{\gamma}{\delta+n}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}h $$

Set this derivative equal to zero: $$ A^{1/(1-\alpha)} \left(\frac{\gamma}{\delta+n}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}h = (1-\gamma)\frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\frac{1}{\gamma} A^{1/(1-\alpha)} \left(\frac{\gamma}{\delta+n}\right)^{\alpha/(1-\alpha)}h $$

$$ 1 = (1-\gamma)\frac{\alpha}{1-\alpha}\frac{1}{\gamma} $$

Then $\gamma^\ast = \alpha$. Steady state consumption maximizing investment rate is equal to $\alpha.$

$\beta$ -convergence: this kind of convergence occurs if the growth rate of GDP per capita is negatively related to the initial level of GDP per capita. That is, $\beta$-convergence is defined by poor countries growing faster than rich countries.

$\sigma$-convergence: this kind of convergence occurs if the variance (or any measure of dispersion, really) of GDP per capita across countries shrinks. $\sigma$-convergence means the gap in GDP per capita from rich to poor gets smaller.

But in the case of economic growth, there are random shocks to GDP per capita. -convergence says that poor countries, on average, grow faster than rich countries. But there will be instances where poor countries have really slow, or negative, growth sometimes. Think of currency crises or political upheavals. There will be some instances where rich countries experience rapid growth. With both of these things going on, its entirely possible that poor countries keep falling farther behind in terms of GDP per capita, -divergence, even though the poorer they get the faster they grow on average.

Convergence speed

$$\frac{\ln y_t - \ln y_0}{t} = \alpha + \beta \ln y_0 $$

To understand the growth rate:

$$ y_t = y_0 e^{gt}$$

. How do you find $g$? $\frac{y_t}{y_0} =e^{gt} $. Then $\ln y_t - \ln y_0 = gt,$ and $g =\frac{\ln y_t - \ln y_0}{t}.$

The size (absolute) of $\beta$ determines the strength of convergence.

$$ \ln y_t = \alpha t + (1+\beta t)\ln y_0 $$

Suppose $ \frac{y_0^{poor}}{y_0^{rich}} = .1.$ For a given $\beta$, how long does it take to reach $ \frac{y_t^{poor}}{y_t^{rich}} = .9?$

$$ \ln y^{rich}_t = \alpha t + (1+\beta t)\ln y^{rich}_0 $$$$ \ln y^{poor}_t = \alpha t + (1+\beta t)\ln y^{poor}_0 $$$$ \ln(.9)+\ln y_t^{rich} = \alpha t + (1+\beta t)(\ln (.1)+\ln y_0^{rich}) $$$$ \ln(.9) + (\alpha t + (1+\beta t)\ln y^{rich}_0) = \alpha t + (1+\beta t)(\ln (.1)+\ln y_0^{rich})$$$$ \ln(.9) = \alpha t + (1+\beta t)(\ln (.1)+\ln y_0^{rich}) - (\alpha t + (1+\beta t)\ln y^{rich}_0) $$$$ \ln(.9) = (1+\beta t)\ln (.1) $$$$ t = \frac{\frac{\ln .9}{\ln .1}-1}{\beta}$$

β¶

0.02 ? Neither. They dont measure the same thing. The former is for unconditional cross-country convergence in the last 25 years, the latter is appropriate for conditional convergence or within-country convergence. Both can be true in their individual setting. Individual provinces within Kenya might converge very quickly, even though Kenya as a whole isnt converging very quickly to U.S. GDP per capita."

It is probably best not to think of unconditional -convergence as representing some kind of structural truth about economic growth. If it were, youd expect it to show up regardless of the exact time frame looked at, and PSS show thats not the case. Its better to think of it as a summary statistic of the general experience of poor countries in a given time frame. The PSS results tell us that something changed around 1990 such that many poor countries grew faster than rich countries, relative to the period from 1960 to 1990. But it doesnt tell us what or why it changed, or if it will persist.
